Abstract

Decision Trees (DTs) are an interpretable knowledge-representation which provides an estimate of a target variable, by posing sequential decisions. They have potential for clinical integration, as clinical guidelines are often presented as DTs. Moreveover, they are already used in guidelines as risk scoring models. Although recent learning algorithms achieve higher predictive accuracy, they do not offer sufficent explanations. As a matter of fact, a novel deep learning approach to cardiovascular event prediction highlights the challenges in understanding the predictions, and stresses the importance of supporting visualizations.

In the clinic, we wish to provide the best possible risk prediction with the smallest possible number of patient measurements. Learning the best DT for a problem is NP-complete. Locally-optimal approaches offer a good trade-off between computational complexity and prediction accuracy, but they do not guarantee a DT which minimizes the decision cost. A DT is a static model, although clinical reality is highly dynamic. An ideal decision support tool is able to adapt to context changes. We propose an algorithm for non-greedy DT learning, which outputs a large space of DTs. The clinician can interact with this output, by selecting the best model for the specific context.
